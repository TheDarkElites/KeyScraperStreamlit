What I meant was this, which seems often overlooked for color-matching muted colors:
More

- Here's your photograph of the pantone chip, which I've very roughly color-corrected with the assumption that the label is "pure white" (more or less):



Using an eyedropper tool to get the color values of the "pink" chip in Photoshop a new window opens showing a color gradient from the measured color value in multiple directions.

Going left-to-right, there's a saturation gradient, and going down top to bottom is a brightness/darkness gradient, which I also colloquially call for the purpose of mixing plastic as "adding gray" to the mix until the result is basically black. 

Your pantone chip has some "gray component" to it already.  I always hope that when GMK sets renders show muted pastel colors, the color matching also focuses on getting a "murky" enough hue to preserve the render look that we use to judge what we might be getting.  Most sets come out more saturated and "clean" than the renders, with less "gray" component by percentage than renders show.  That more murky pastel is what gives the more muted vintagey color, as opposed to a "clean" pale peachy pink.  To illustrate that, here's the same photograph of the pantone chip with a square of new color overlaid on top, with less "gray" and more saturation:



This illustrates the difference I very frequently see between renders and finished product: higher saturation and less of the gray component.  I'd say most sets suffer from this inconsistency.

What I meant was this, which seems often overlooked for color-matching muted colors:
More

- Here's your photograph of the pantone chip, which I've very roughly color-corrected with the assumption that the label is "pure white" (more or less):

Show Image


Using an eyedropper tool to get the color values of the "pink" chip in Photoshop a new window opens showing a color gradient from the measured color value in multiple directions.

Going left-to-right, there's a saturation gradient, and going down top to bottom is a brightness/darkness gradient, which I also colloquially call for the purpose of mixing plastic as "adding gray" to the mix until the result is basically black. 

Your pantone chip has some "gray component" to it already.  I always hope that when GMK sets renders show muted pastel colors, the color matching also focuses on getting a "murky" enough hue to preserve the render look that we use to judge what we might be getting.  Most sets come out more saturated and "clean" than the renders, with less "gray" component by percentage than renders show.  That more murky pastel is what gives the more muted vintagey color, as opposed to a "clean" pale peachy pink.  To illustrate that, here's the same photograph of the pantone chip with a square of new color overlaid on top, with less "gray" and more saturation:

Show Image


This illustrates the difference I very frequently see between renders and finished product: higher saturation and less of the gray component.  I'd say most sets suffer from this inconsistency.

You can often find RGB by just Googling the color code.  This might help you - https://www.pantone.com/color-finder/PQ-13-1406TCX
